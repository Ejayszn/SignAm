Landlord Keeping Your Caution Deposit? Legal Steps for Nigerian Tenants

In Nigeria, caution deposits are frequently treated by landlords as bonus income rather than refundable money held in trust.

By default, unless physical damage is explicitly proven, your caution fee must be returned to you at the expiration of your tenancy. Sadly, thousands of tenants lose this money because their tenancy agreements were vague, incomplete, or entirely verbal.

The key difference between tenants who recover their deposit and those who do not is evidence. A clear contract, dated receipts, inspection photos, and written communication create a record that makes it hard for a landlord to simply keep your money.

Is a landlord legally required to return a caution deposit in Nigeria?

Yes—unless the tenancy agreement explicitly states otherwise or damage beyond normal wear and tear has occurred. Most state tenancy laws, including Lagos Tenancy Law 2011, recognise that the caution deposit is refundable money held against abuse, unpaid rent, or damage. If the landlord keeps the deposit without a lawful reason, they can be challenged in court.

Keep in mind that the legal test is not whether the landlord says they are “keeping” it, but whether they can prove a valid claim: unpaid rent, repairs caused by the tenant, or another obligation in the agreement.

Why landlords withhold caution deposits

  • No written condition report: The landlord claims the property was damaged, but there is no documented pre-move-in record.
  • Unclear refund timeline: The contract says “deposit refundable” without a deadline, leaving the tenant chasing the refund forever.
  • Unpaid rent or bills: Landlords sometimes deduct from the deposit for rent arrears or unpaid utility charges that were not clearly part of the agreement.
  • Cash payments and poor receipts: No bank transfer means no official proof of payment in a dispute.

How to make your deposit claim strong

Use these steps before you move in, while you live there, and when you move out.

  • Write a proper tenancy agreement: Include the exact amount of the deposit, what it covers, the refund timeline, and any possible deductions. If the landlord refuses, insist on at least a receipt and a signed note of the terms.
  • Photograph every room: Take time-stamped photos or video of the property before you move in and keep a copy. Do the same on the day you hand the keys back.
  • Keep all payment proof: Bank transfers, POS receipts, payment alerts, email confirmations and any signed receipt with the amount and date.
  • Manage repairs and cleaning: For minor wear and tear, clean and document the condition so the landlord has no ground for unfair deductions.
  • Preserve communication: Save text messages, WhatsApp chats, emails, and letters that refer to the deposit and the move-out process.

What to do if your landlord still refuses

Start with a written demand. A clear Letter of Demand should state:

  • Your name and the property address.
  • The amount of the caution deposit paid.
  • The date you moved out.
  • The number of days you expect the refund.
  • A deadline of 7–14 days for payment before you take further steps.

If the landlord ignores the demand, escalate the matter.

In most states, the next step is the Rental Tribunal, Rent Tribunal, or Magistrate Court. These bodies can hear deposit disputes quickly and usually at low cost. Take everything with you:

  • Tenancy agreement or memorandum of terms.
  • Proof of deposit payment (bank transfer, POS receipt, or signed acknowledgement).
  • Move-in and move-out photos or video.
  • Inspection reports and the handover checklist.
  • Copies of any written correspondence, including demand letters.

Court and tribunal officers are more likely to rule for tenants when the evidence is organised, chronological, and directly linked to the terms of the agreement.

What if the landlord claims “damage beyond normal wear and tear”?

Normal wear and tear is the ordinary deterioration that happens through everyday use, like faded paint, worn edges on a wooden door, or light carpet marks. The landlord must show actual damage that goes beyond this, such as broken windows, large holes in walls, or lost fixtures.

Without a pre-move-in inspection note, you can contest any claim that is vague or unsupported. If the repair costs seem excessive, ask for a repair invoice or contractor estimate in writing.

When to seek legal help

If the landlord still refuses after a demand letter, and you believe your evidence is strong, consulting a lawyer or tenant rights organisation can help you choose the right forum and avoid avoidable delays. Many tribunals also offer mediation first, which can lead to a faster settlement.

Remember, the goal is to recover the deposit, not to turn the dispute into a long legal fight. Well-documented evidence and a clear process give you the best chance of a fast return.
