How to Sue Someone in Small Claims Court in Lagos

Most Nigerians believe that taking someone to court requires a lawyer, months of waiting, and money you probably do not have. For disputes under ₦5 million, Lagos Small Claims Court was specifically built to prove that wrong.

The Small Claims Court in Lagos is designed for everyday people — business owners, freelancers, landlords, and individuals — to resolve financial disputes quickly and without legal representation. If someone owes you money and has refused to pay after a demand, this is your clearest path to a judgment.

What kinds of disputes can Small Claims Court handle?

The Lagos Small Claims Court handles civil money disputes, including:

  • Unpaid loans between individuals or businesses.
  • Unpaid invoices for goods supplied or services rendered.
  • Refusal to return a deposit or caution fee.
  • Breach of a simple contract or service agreement.
  • Disputes arising from sale of goods where payment was not made.

The court does not handle criminal matters, family disputes, land ownership, or claims above ₦5,000,000. For those, you need the Magistrate Court or High Court.

Step 1: Prepare your evidence before you file

Filing without evidence is a waste of your filing fee and your time. Before you approach the court registry, gather everything that proves the debt exists and was not paid:

  • Bank transfer receipts showing the money you sent or were owed.
  • WhatsApp or SMS conversations where the amount and terms were discussed.
  • Any signed agreement, invoice, or receipt.
  • Your Letter of Demand and proof that it was delivered.
  • Any debt acknowledgement letter the debtor may have signed.

Print everything in at least three copies: one for the court, one for the defendant, and one for yourself.

Step 2: Write your Statement of Claim

The Statement of Claim is a short document — usually one to two pages — that tells the court who you are, who you are suing, what happened, and what you want. It does not need to be written in legal language. Write it clearly and factually:

  • Your full name, address, and phone number.
  • The defendant's full name, address, and phone number.
  • A brief factual account of the transaction — what you supplied, when, and for how much.
  • What the defendant agreed to pay and when.
  • What remains unpaid as of today.
  • What you are asking the court to order — typically, full payment of the outstanding amount.
Tip: Keep your Statement of Claim factual and unemotional. The court is not interested in how you feel about the situation. It wants to know what happened, what was agreed, and what was not paid.

Step 3: File at the court registry

Lagos Small Claims Courts operate within the Magistrate Court system. You can file at the Magistrate Court closest to either your address or the defendant's address. Key locations include the Magistrate Courts in Ikeja, Lagos Island, Badagry, Epe, and Ikorodu.

At the registry, you will:

  • Submit your Statement of Claim with all attached evidence.
  • Pay the filing fee. The fee is calculated based on the value of your claim and is typically a small percentage of the amount you are claiming.
  • Receive a case number and a hearing date.

The court will serve the defendant with a copy of your claim and a summons to appear. If the defendant cannot be located, inform the registry clerk so they can advise on substituted service.

Step 4: Attend the hearing

On the hearing day, arrive early and bring all your original documents plus your copies. The magistrate will hear both sides. You will be asked to explain your claim, present your evidence, and respond to anything the defendant says.

Speak clearly and stick to facts. If the defendant does not appear, the court will typically enter judgment in your favour by default after confirming that they were properly served.

What happens after judgment?

If the court rules in your favour, it will issue a judgment order stating the amount the defendant must pay. If the defendant still refuses, you can apply for enforcement through the same court. The two main enforcement tools are:

  • Garnishee order: The court directs the defendant's bank to pay you directly from their account.
  • Warrant of execution: The court bailiff can seize and sell the defendant's moveable assets to satisfy the judgment.

The enforcement process takes additional time, but a court judgment significantly changes the debtor's position. Ignoring a judgment carries serious legal consequences that casual debt does not.

How to build the strongest possible case

The outcome of a small claims case almost always comes down to documentation. A claimant with clear bank records, a signed agreement, and a delivered Letter of Demand is in a far stronger position than one who relied on verbal promises and WhatsApp messages that were later deleted.

For future transactions, use a proper digital agreement before money changes hands. When both parties have signed a verified contract, the dispute rarely needs to reach the court at all — the signed record usually prompts the debtor to settle before a hearing date is set.
