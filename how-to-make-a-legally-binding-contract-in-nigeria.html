How to Make a Legally Binding Contract in Nigeria

One of the biggest myths in Nigeria is that an agreement is only valid if it was drafted by a lawyer, printed on thick paper, or decorated with a red wax seal.

The truth is, under Nigerian contract law, legal validity depends on the substance of the deal, not the thickness of the paper or the colour of the ink. Whether you are partnering on a business, selling a phone, hiring a contractor, or renting out an apartment, you can create a legally binding contract yourself—if you follow a few core legal rules.

Nigeria follows the basic common-law contract principles adopted from English law. That means courts look for a clear exchange of promises, a lawful purpose, and evidence that both parties intended the arrangement to be enforceable.

What makes an agreement legally valid in Nigeria?

To be enforceable in court, a contract normally needs these essential elements:

  • Offer: One party must make a definite proposal. For example, "I will deliver 100 branded flyers to you for ₦80,000." A vague promise like "I will help you later" is not a valid offer.
  • Acceptance: The other party must accept the exact terms of the offer. If the recipient changes a key term, that is a counter-offer, not acceptance.
  • Consideration: Something of value must be exchanged. It can be money, goods, services, or even a promise to act or not act. The law does not require the amount to be fair, only that there is some measurable value.
  • Intention to Create Legal Relations: In commercial deals, courts usually assume the parties intend to be bound. In social or family arrangements, the intention must be clearer.
  • Capacity: Parties must have the legal ability to contract. Minors, persons of unsound mind, and intoxicated individuals may lack capacity, making the contract voidable.
  • Lawful Purpose: The agreement must not require either party to do something illegal. A contract to sell stolen property is void from the start.

With these elements in place, even a simple written note, an email thread, or a signed digital agreement can be enforceable.

Does a contract have to be written?

Technically, no. Nigerian courts recognise oral contracts, and many everyday agreements are legally binding even without a document. The issue is evidence. If a dispute emerges, an oral deal becomes a memory contest.

Writing the contract gives you a fixed record of the parties, the obligation, the price, the delivery date, the payment terms, and any conditions. It is the strongest way to avoid confusion and to show the court what was agreed.

Which agreements still should be written?

Some contracts are best and sometimes required to be in writing. In Nigeria, those often include:

  • Sale or transfer of land and lease agreements, which usually need written evidence before land registry officials.
  • Guarantees and suretyship arrangements, where one person promises to pay another's debt.
  • Contracts that must be registered under specific laws, such as certain company or banking agreements.

Even if a contract is valid orally, a written record makes enforcement easier and helps settle disagreements quickly.

Can a digital contract stand up in a Nigerian court?

Yes. The Nigerian Evidence Act 2011 recognises electronic records and electronic signatures as admissible evidence, provided they are reliable and properly linked to the signatory.

For a digital agreement to be strong, it should show:

  • Who signed it, with verifiable identity information.
  • When and how it was signed.
  • That the terms were not altered after signing.

That is why a trusted digital contract platform is valuable. It captures the signing process, stores the final agreement securely, and gives you an audit trail if a dispute arises.

How to prepare a stronger contract

Follow these practical steps to make your agreement clearer and harder to challenge:

  • Identify the parties by full name and contact details.
  • Describe the obligation clearly: what is being delivered, by when, and under what conditions.
  • State the price or consideration, including payment method and schedule.
  • Include a delivery or completion date, and clarify what happens if one party fails to perform.
  • Add a jurisdiction or governing law clause, for example, that disputes will be resolved under Nigerian law in a specific state.

When terms are precise, a judge can enforce the contract. When terms are vague, the agreement may be treated as too uncertain to enforce.

Common mistakes to avoid

Some of the most common mistakes are:

  • Using unclear or incomplete descriptions of what each party must do.
  • Assuming there is no contract because the parties are family or friends.
  • Failing to confirm the identity of the other party before signing.
  • Relying only on WhatsApp or SMS messages without summarising the final terms in a proper agreement.

By avoiding these errors, you make the contract easier to enforce and easier to rely on if the relationship breaks down.
