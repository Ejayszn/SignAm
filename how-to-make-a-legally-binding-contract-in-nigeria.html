How to Make a Legally Binding Contract in Nigeria

One of the biggest myths in Nigeria is that an agreement is only valid if it was drafted by a lawyer, printed on thick paper, or decorated with a red wax seal.

The truth is, under Nigerian contract law, legal validity depends on the substance of the deal, not the thickness of the paper or the colour of the ink. Whether you are partnering on a business, selling a phone, hiring a contractor, or renting out an apartment, you can create a legally binding contract yourself — if you follow a few core legal rules.

Nigeria follows the basic common-law contract principles adopted from English law. That means courts look for a clear exchange of promises, a lawful purpose, and evidence that both parties intended the arrangement to be enforceable. A court does not ask whether a lawyer was present. It asks whether a valid agreement was made.

What makes an agreement legally valid in Nigeria?

To be enforceable in court, a contract normally needs these essential elements:

  • Offer: One party must make a definite proposal. For example, "I will deliver 100 branded flyers to you for ₦80,000." A vague promise like "I will help you later" is not a valid offer because it lacks the specificity a court can hold you to.
  • Acceptance: The other party must accept the exact terms of the offer. If the recipient changes a key term — such as the price or delivery date — that is a counter-offer, not acceptance, and the original offer no longer stands.
  • Consideration: Something of value must be exchanged. It can be money, goods, services, or even a promise to act or not act. The law does not require the amount to be fair, only that there is some measurable exchange of value between the parties.
  • Intention to Create Legal Relations: In commercial deals, courts usually assume the parties intend to be bound. In social or family arrangements, the intention must be clearer and is harder to prove if challenged.
  • Capacity: Parties must have the legal ability to contract. Minors, persons of unsound mind, and intoxicated individuals may lack capacity, making the contract voidable at the weaker party's option.
  • Lawful Purpose: The agreement must not require either party to do something illegal or contrary to public policy. A contract to sell stolen property, for example, is void from the start and cannot be enforced by either side.

With these elements in place, even a simple written note, a clear email thread, or a signed digital agreement can be fully enforceable.

Does a contract have to be written?

Technically, no. Nigerian courts recognise oral contracts, and many everyday agreements are legally binding even without a document. A handshake deal at the market can be a contract. The problem is always evidence.

If a dispute emerges, an oral deal becomes a memory contest — your word against theirs, with no timestamp, no signed terms, and no way to prove who agreed to what. Courts can only work with what you can show them. A written agreement gives you a fixed record of the parties, the obligation, the price, the delivery date, the payment terms, and any special conditions. It is the strongest and simplest way to avoid confusion and to show the court exactly what was agreed.

Even a brief, clearly worded document is far better than nothing. Courts have enforced agreements written on the back of a receipt when the terms were unambiguous and the signatures clear.

Which agreements must be in writing in Nigeria?

While many contracts can be oral, some categories are best — and sometimes legally required — to be documented in writing. In Nigeria, those include:

  • Sale or transfer of land and long-term lease agreements, which usually need written evidence before land registry officials will act on them.
  • Guarantees and suretyship arrangements, where one person promises to pay another's debt if they default.
  • Contracts that must be registered under specific statutes, such as certain company, mortgage, or banking agreements regulated by the Companies and Allied Matters Act or the Central Bank of Nigeria.
  • Hire-purchase and credit sale agreements, where writing is required under the Hire Purchase Act.

Even when writing is not strictly required by law, a written record makes enforcement faster, settlement easier, and the parties' obligations clearer from the start.

Can a digital contract stand up in a Nigerian court?

Yes. The Nigerian Evidence Act 2011 recognises electronic records and electronic signatures as admissible evidence in court, provided they are reliable and properly linked to the signatory. This means an agreement signed digitally carries the same potential legal weight as a paper document — sometimes more, because the signing process can be independently verified.

For a digital agreement to hold up, it should clearly establish:

  • Who signed it, with verifiable identity information that links the signature to a real, traceable individual.
  • When and how it was signed, including a reliable timestamp.
  • That the terms were not altered after signing, through a tamper-evident or encrypted record.

This is why the method of signing matters as much as the content. A WhatsApp message saying "yes I agree" is technically evidence but easy to repudiate — the other party can claim their phone was accessed by someone else, or that they did not understand what they were agreeing to. A structured digital signing process that ties the signature to verified identity removes that avenue of escape entirely.

Note on the Evidence Act: Section 84 of the Evidence Act 2011 specifically provides for the admissibility of computer-generated documents, including electronic agreements, where it can be shown that the computer or device was operating properly at the relevant time and that the document was produced in the ordinary course of activities. A reputable digital contract platform satisfies this automatically.

How to prepare a stronger contract

Whether you are drafting a one-page agreement yourself or using a digital platform, the strength of your contract comes down to clarity and completeness. Follow these steps for every transaction that matters:

  • Identify the parties precisely. Use full legal names, not nicknames or usernames. Include phone numbers and addresses. This matters if you ever need to serve a court process.
  • Describe the obligation clearly. What is being delivered, by when, and under what conditions? A contract that says "I will provide social media management" is too vague. One that says "I will post five graphics per week to three platforms for three months starting 1 July 2026" is enforceable.
  • State the price and payment schedule. Include the total amount, deposit if any, balance payment date, payment method, and what happens if a payment is late.
  • Specify consequences for non-performance. What happens if the contractor misses the deadline? What happens if the client refuses to pay? A clause spelling this out removes ambiguity and discourages default.
  • Add a governing law and dispute resolution clause. State that disputes will be resolved under Nigerian law, in a specific state, or through a named arbitration process. This prevents arguments about which rules apply.
  • Both parties must sign. An unsigned contract is a draft. Ensure both parties have confirmed their acceptance in a verifiable way before any money or goods change hands.

What can make a contract void or unenforceable?

Understanding what kills a contract is just as important as knowing how to build one. Nigerian courts may refuse to enforce an agreement in the following situations:

  • Missing essential elements: If there is no clear offer, no acceptance, or no consideration, the court may find there was never a contract at all.
  • Terms too vague to interpret: A contract that says "payment will be made soon" gives the court nothing to enforce. Vague terms are routinely struck out or treated as unenforceable.
  • Duress or undue influence: If one party was pressured, threatened, or manipulated into signing, the contract may be voidable. This is especially relevant where there is a clear power imbalance between the parties.
  • Misrepresentation: If one party lied about a material fact — for example, claiming goods were brand new when they were refurbished — the other party may have the right to rescind the contract.
  • Illegality: Any agreement requiring an illegal act is void. Courts will not help either party to enforce it, even the party who was cheated.
  • Lack of capacity: Contracts with minors under 18 are generally unenforceable against the minor, with limited exceptions for necessities.

Knowing these pitfalls helps you draft agreements that are tight from the start, leaving the other party with fewer grounds to walk away.

Common mistakes Nigerians make when contracting

Most disputes that end up in small claims courts or magistrate courts in Nigeria could have been avoided with a properly prepared agreement. The most common errors are:

  • Relying entirely on WhatsApp or SMS messages without summarising the final agreed terms in a separate document that both parties have signed.
  • Assuming a family or friendship relationship removes the need for documentation. In practice, family disputes over money are some of the hardest to resolve precisely because there is usually no written record.
  • Paying money before a contract is signed. Once cash has moved, your leverage to get terms confirmed drops sharply.
  • Failing to confirm the other party's identity before entering a deal. Contracts signed by someone using a false name or someone else's credentials create serious enforcement problems.
  • Using a template that does not reflect what was actually agreed. A mismatch between the signed document and the real understanding of the parties is almost as bad as having no document at all.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a contract need to be written to be valid in Nigeria? +
No. Nigerian courts recognise oral contracts. The problem is evidence — if a dispute arises, an unwritten agreement becomes a memory contest. A written contract gives you a fixed record that a court can actually enforce.
Is a digital or electronic contract valid in Nigeria? +
Yes. The Nigerian Evidence Act 2011 recognises electronic records and electronic signatures as admissible evidence, provided they are reliable and properly linked to the signatory. A trusted digital contract platform that captures identity, timestamp, and a tamper-proof audit trail gives you the strongest possible record.
Do I need a lawyer to write a contract in Nigeria? +
Not necessarily. For most everyday business transactions — freelance work, loans between individuals, sale of goods, service agreements — you do not need a lawyer to draft a valid contract. What you need is a clear, written record of the offer, acceptance, consideration, and agreed terms, signed by both parties.
What makes a contract void or unenforceable in Nigeria? +
A contract can be void or unenforceable if it lacks essential elements like offer, acceptance, or consideration; if the purpose is illegal; if one party lacked capacity (e.g. a minor); if the terms are too vague for a court to interpret; or if one party was coerced or misled into signing.
