How to Write a Debt Acknowledgement Letter in Nigeria

When a debtor verbally admits they owe you money but keeps delaying payment, a debt acknowledgement letter turns that admission into something a court can actually use.

Many creditors in Nigeria have strong evidence — bank transfers, WhatsApp messages, delivery records — but still lose their claims because the debtor claims the payment was a gift, a different transaction, or simply never happened. A signed debt acknowledgement letter eliminates that argument entirely. It is a document where the debtor confirms, in writing, that they owe you a specific amount.

What is a debt acknowledgement letter?

A debt acknowledgement letter is a written statement signed by the person who owes money, confirming the existence of the debt. It is different from a loan agreement, which you sign before the money changes hands. The acknowledgement letter is signed after the debt already exists, usually when the creditor needs stronger documentation or when the debtor has started making excuses.

Under Nigerian contract and evidence law, a signed written acknowledgement carries significant weight. It can also restart the clock under the Limitation Law — meaning that even if several years have passed since the original transaction, a fresh acknowledgement gives the creditor new legal standing to pursue the debt.

When should you request a debt acknowledgement letter?

You should request one as soon as you sense the debtor is becoming evasive. Specific situations where it is particularly useful include:

  • The original agreement was verbal and you have no signed contract.
  • The debtor is still cooperative but has started missing payment deadlines.
  • You are restructuring the repayment plan and want the new terms documented.
  • A significant amount of time has passed since the original transaction.
  • The debtor has admitted the debt in WhatsApp messages but you want a formal record.

The best time to get one signed is when the relationship is still cordial. Once the debtor has decided to fully ignore you, getting a signature becomes much harder.

What to include in the letter

Keep the document simple and direct. The more straightforward it is, the harder it is for the debtor to claim they misunderstood what they signed. The letter should include:

  • Full names and contact details of both the creditor and the debtor, including phone numbers.
  • The exact amount owed in figures and words, with the currency clearly stated in Naira.
  • The date the debt was incurred and a brief description of what it was for — a loan, goods supplied, services rendered, or a deposit paid.
  • Confirmation that the amount remains unpaid as of the date of signing.
  • A repayment commitment — either a lump sum date or a structured instalment plan with specific dates and amounts.
  • Consequences for non-payment — a simple statement that failure to pay by the agreed date gives the creditor the right to pursue legal recovery.
  • The debtor's signature, printed name, and the date of signing.
Important: Do not make the letter sound aggressive or accusatory. The goal is to get a signature, not to start a fight. A calm, factual tone is far more likely to result in the debtor signing the document.

Sample debt acknowledgement letter format

Below is a simple format you can adapt. Replace the bracketed sections with the actual details:

DEBT ACKNOWLEDGEMENT LETTER

Date: [Date of signing]

I, [Debtor's Full Name], with phone number [Phone Number], hereby acknowledge that I owe [Creditor's Full Name] the sum of ₦[Amount in figures] ([Amount in words] Naira only.

This debt arose from [brief description — e.g. a personal loan received on [date] / goods supplied on [date] / services rendered on [date]].

I confirm that as of the date of this letter, the full amount of ₦[Amount] remains unpaid.

I commit to repaying this amount as follows: [lump sum on [date] / instalments of ₦[amount] on the [day] of each month, beginning [start date]].

I understand that failure to honour this repayment plan gives [Creditor's Full Name] the right to pursue legal recovery of the full outstanding amount without further notice.

Signed: ________________________

Printed Name: ________________________

Date: ________________________

Is a debt acknowledgement letter legally binding in Nigeria?

Yes, provided it is signed voluntarily and the terms are clear. Nigerian courts have consistently recognised signed acknowledgements of debt as enforceable records. The key requirement is that the debtor must sign freely, without coercion, and must have understood what they were signing.

A digitally signed acknowledgement that includes the debtor's verified phone number and a recorded timestamp is even stronger, because it is harder to claim that the signature was forged or that the document was altered after the fact.

What if the debtor refuses to sign?

If the debtor refuses, document the refusal itself. Send the letter by WhatsApp or email so you have a record of them receiving it and declining. Their refusal does not cancel the debt, but it does signal that you need to escalate.

From that point, rely on your secondary evidence — bank alerts, chat history, invoices — and proceed to send a formal Letter of Demand, then file in Small Claims Court if necessary. The refusal to sign can itself be presented as evidence of bad faith.

How to make your acknowledgement tamper-proof

A paper letter can be lost, altered, or denied. A digital acknowledgement that captures the debtor's identity through their verified phone number — and stores the signed record securely — is far harder to dispute. When both parties sign through a platform that records the signing event, date, and identity, you have something that stands up even if the debtor later claims they never agreed.

Before any money changes hands in future, consider using a proper agreement from the start rather than relying on an after-the-fact acknowledgement. Prevention is far easier than recovery.
