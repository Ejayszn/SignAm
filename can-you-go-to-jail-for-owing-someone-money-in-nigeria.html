Can You Go to Jail for Owing Someone Money in Nigeria?

This is one of the most searched questions about debt in Nigeria, and the answer depends entirely on how the money was obtained in the first place.

The short answer is: you generally cannot go to jail in Nigeria simply for owing someone money. Debt is a civil matter, not a criminal one. But there are important exceptions — and the line between owing money and committing fraud is one that many Nigerians do not fully understand until they are standing on the wrong side of it.

Civil debt versus criminal fraud

Nigerian law draws a clear line between two very different situations:

Civil debt is when someone genuinely owes money — from a loan, unpaid invoice, or broken agreement — but has failed to repay it. The creditor's remedy is a civil lawsuit. If they win, the court issues a judgment ordering payment. The debtor does not go to prison for the debt itself.

Criminal fraud is when someone used deception, forged documents, fake identities, or false pretences to obtain money they never had any intention of repaying. This is a criminal offence under the Nigerian Criminal Code Act, specifically under sections dealing with obtaining property by false pretences. This can result in arrest, prosecution, and imprisonment.

The distinction matters enormously. If a friend lent you money and you have genuinely struggled to repay it, that is a civil matter. If you told someone you had goods to sell, collected payment, and there were never any goods — that is fraud.

What the police can and cannot do

The Nigerian Police Force does not have the authority to arrest someone purely for owing a civil debt. When creditors run to the police station to report a debtor, the police should — and in most well-run stations, will — refer the matter to court. An ordinary debt dispute is not a criminal matter.

However, the reality in Nigeria is more complicated. Some police officers, particularly at divisional stations, accept petitions on debt matters and may invite, detain, or intimidate debtors on behalf of complainants. This practice is technically outside the proper bounds of the law, but it happens.

If you are a debtor who has been arrested or threatened with arrest purely over an unpaid civil debt, you have the right to challenge that detention. A lawyer can file for your release on the basis that no criminal offence was committed.

Important: If you lent money to someone and they used fake details, forged transfer alerts, or clearly had no intention of repaying from the start, report it both to the police and to the EFCC. That is a fraud case, not a debt collection case, and the criminal route becomes available to you.

Can contempt of court lead to imprisonment?

Yes — and this is the most important exception that creditors should understand. While a debtor cannot be jailed for the debt itself, once a court issues a judgment ordering payment and the debtor willfully ignores it, they can be held in contempt of court.

Contempt of a court order is a separate offence. The creditor can apply to the court to enforce the judgment, and if the debtor continues to defy the order, the judge has the power to impose fines or, in serious cases, a period of imprisonment until the order is complied with.

This is why pursuing your debt through the court system, rather than informal pressure, ultimately gives you more power as a creditor.

The EFCC and debt disputes

Many Nigerians file petitions with the Economic and Financial Crimes Commission over debt matters. The EFCC's mandate covers financial crimes, and they do sometimes act on debt-related complaints — particularly where there is an element of fraud, advance fee schemes, or deliberate deceit.

If your debtor used forged documents, a fake identity, or a bounced cheque to obtain money from you, the EFCC is a legitimate route. For an ordinary unpaid loan or business debt with no fraudulent element, the civil courts are the correct path.

How a proper agreement changes your position

Whether you are a creditor or a debtor, a clearly signed agreement is your best protection. For creditors, a document that captures the debtor's verified identity makes it much easier to prove the debt and pursue recovery through the courts. For debtors acting in good faith, a signed agreement that documents genuine repayment difficulties is evidence that the matter is civil, not criminal.

The cases where people end up in serious legal trouble are almost always the ones where nothing was written down, the terms were vague, and neither party can prove what was actually agreed. Clear documentation protects everyone.
