Vendor Agreement Guide for Nigerian Events and Small Businesses

Nigerian event vendors—caterers, MCs, decorators, makeup artists, photographers—are highly vulnerable to sudden planning adjustments and outright cancellations.

When an event date gets shifted or canceled entirely, a simple verbal chat about deposits leads straight to bad blood, heated arguments, and social media call-outs. Without a written record, you are dependent on memory, trust, and goodwill.

That is why a strong vendor agreement is not just paperwork; it is the difference between a business you can run with confidence and one where every booking feels like a gamble.

Why a written agreement matters

In our market, people often assume that a deposit or a WhatsApp message is enough. It is not. A proper agreement does four practical things for you:

  • Clarifies what you will deliver and when, so clients cannot claim you did not understand the job.
  • Connects the deposit to a calendar date, not just to the client’s promise.
  • Creates an enforceable record for disputes, backed by Nigerian law when the terms are clear.
  • Reduces the chance of arguments because everyone signs the same document.

What to include in every vendor agreement

Every agreement should answer these basic questions in plain language:

  • Who is responsible – full legal names, business names, phone numbers, and email addresses for both sides.
  • What exactly is being provided – number of plates, hours of service, number of photos, makeup stations, décor items, delivery time, and scope of setup.
  • When the event happens – the date, expected start and finish times, and any setup or teardown windows.
  • Where it takes place – full venue name and address, including any alternate location details if needed.
  • How much is due – retainer deposit amount, balance amount, payment schedule, and payment methods.
  • What changes cost – postponement fee, cancellation fee, and how much notice is required.

1. Non-refundable retainer fee

Use clear wording like:

"The retainer deposit is non-refundable because the agreed event date is held exclusively for the client and prevents the vendor from accepting other bookings."

This is not just a sentence; it is the foundation of your cancellation rights. If your calendar is blocked for one or two days, you cannot simply take another job for the same date.

2. Postponement and rescheduling rules

Most disputes come when the client wants to move the date. Your contract should distinguish between:

  • Early notice reschedule – a small administration fee when the change is made early enough.
  • Late reschedule – a larger percentage of the total price when the move is within 30 days of the event.
  • Cancellation – when the client decides not to go ahead at all.

Example clause:

"If the client requests a new event date more than 30 days before the original date, the client pays 10% of the agreed service fee as a rescheduling fee. If the change is requested within 30 days, the client pays 50% of the agreed service fee."

3. Force majeure and responsibility limits

In Nigeria, local emergencies can disrupt events: heavy rain, power outage, fuel scarcity, state restrictions, or road closures. A force majeure clause should explain:

  • Which events qualify as beyond both parties' control.
  • What happens to payments already made.
  • Whether the vendor will try to offer a new date or return part of the balance.

Good phrasing keeps it simple: when an event is canceled for a reason outside the vendor’s control, the retainer is retained and the parties will discuss either a new date or a partial refund based on the work already done.

4. Scope of work and deliverables

It is easy to overlook small details. A proper agreement prevents scope creep by listing the deliverables explicitly.

  • For caterers: number of guests, menu type, service style, equipment rental, and clean-up responsibility.
  • For photographers: coverage hours, number of edited images, delivery format, and album or digital file deadlines.
  • For decorators: exact décor items, set-up and takedown times, backup plan for fragile pieces, and whether you provide extension cords or ladders.
  • For makeup artists: number of clients, service location, products provided, and time per person.

How to talk to clients who say "we are family"

The answer is simple: stay professional and explain that the agreement is for everyone's protection. Say it like this:

"I respect our relationship, so I want to make sure we both understand the plan clearly. This form just protects us if anything changes and avoids stress for everyone."

Most clients will appreciate that you are being fair rather than informal. If they still resist, point to the cost of your time, the deposit, and the fact that the date is already reserved for them.

When a client cancels last minute

If a booking is canceled within the window defined in your contract, follow these steps:

  • Send the client a written notice quoting the cancellation clause.
  • Confirm how much of the deposit you are entitled to keep.
  • Invoice any additional expenses already incurred, such as materials bought or staff assigned.

With a signed, dated agreement, you are not asking for a favor. You are enforcing the terms the client agreed to.

Using digital contracts to make this easier

Digital vendor agreements are faster and more secure than paper. A system that records client identity, time stamps signature acceptance, and stores the agreement in the cloud gives you a reliable record that can be shared with courts or enforcement agencies if needed.

For Nigerian vendors, that means fewer disputes, less debate over what was said, and stronger control over your income.
