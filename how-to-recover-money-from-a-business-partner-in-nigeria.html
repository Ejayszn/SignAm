How to Recover Money from a Business Partner in Nigeria

Business partnership disputes are some of the most financially damaging and emotionally draining situations a Nigerian entrepreneur can face — because the person who owes you money was also the person you trusted.

Whether the partnership broke down because your partner withdrew more than their share, disappeared with the capital, stopped contributing while collecting profits, or simply refuses to account for shared funds, the recovery process follows a clear legal path. The challenge is that most Nigerian business partnerships are informal, started on trust and a handshake — which makes documentation the first and most critical issue to address.

Step 1: Reconstruct the financial record

Before you approach anyone — your partner, a mediator, or a court — you need to know exactly what you are owed and be able to prove it. This means going through every financial record connected to the business:

  • Bank transfer records showing your capital contributions, operating transfers, and any withdrawals by either party.
  • WhatsApp, SMS, or email conversations where profit-sharing, contributions, or financial obligations were discussed.
  • Any written agreements, even informal ones — a shared document, a voice note describing terms, or a chat thread where both parties agreed to specific arrangements.
  • Invoices, receipts, and supplier records that show the business's actual transactions.
  • Social media posts, business registration documents, or any public record that acknowledges the partnership.

The goal is to construct a clear, chronological record of what money went in, what was agreed, and what your partner owes you. This record is the foundation of everything that follows.

Step 2: Request a formal account from your partner

Before escalating, give your partner the opportunity to account for the funds in writing. Send a clear, professional message — preferably through WhatsApp or email so it is documented — requesting a full account of the business finances, including any amounts they believe are outstanding on either side.

Their response, or silence, becomes part of your evidence. A partner who acknowledges a debt in writing has essentially given you a debt acknowledgement. A partner who goes silent or becomes evasive is building a case for you to take to court.

Important: Keep all communication professional and in writing from this point. Avoid heated verbal arguments. Every message sent in anger can be used against you; every calm, documented request strengthens your position.

Step 3: Attempt mediation before litigation

Business partnership disputes are often resolved faster through mediation than through court proceedings. A neutral third party — a respected mutual contact, a professional mediator, or a dispute resolution service — can help both parties reach a settlement without the cost and time of a lawsuit.

If you reach a mediated settlement, document it immediately in a formal written agreement signed by both parties. A verbal resolution at a mediation session is worth nothing if your partner reneges a week later.

Step 4: Issue a formal demand and file in court if necessary

If mediation fails or your partner refuses to engage, send a formal Letter of Demand stating the specific amount you are claiming, the basis for the claim, and a deadline for payment or response. Deliver it by a trackable method.

If the amount is within the jurisdiction of the Small Claims Court (up to ₦5 million in Lagos), you can file there without a lawyer. For larger amounts, or disputes involving complex business accounts, a Magistrate Court or the High Court with legal representation may be necessary.

In your Statement of Claim, be specific: state the total you contributed, what was agreed in terms of returns or repayment, and what remains outstanding. Attach all your documentary evidence.

What if there was no written partnership agreement?

This is the most common situation in Nigeria, and it does not automatically mean you lose your claim. Courts will look at the totality of the evidence to determine what was agreed. Bank transfers showing your contributions, chat logs discussing the business, and any acknowledgement of the arrangement by your partner — all of this can establish that a partnership existed and that money was owed.

However, cases without written agreements are harder, slower, and more expensive to litigate. The lesson for future partnerships is clear: document everything before you invest a single naira.

How to protect yourself in future business partnerships

A partnership agreement does not have to be a long legal document. At minimum, it should capture:

  • Each partner's name, contact details, and contribution (cash, assets, or services).
  • The profit and loss sharing arrangement and how withdrawals are managed.
  • Decision-making authority — who can spend what without the other's approval.
  • What happens when one partner wants to exit or when the partnership dissolves.
  • A clear dispute resolution clause specifying how disagreements will be handled.

A digitally signed agreement that captures each partner's verified identity creates a tamper-proof record from day one. It also signals to your partner from the start that you take the arrangement seriously — which often deters casual exploitation of the relationship.
