How to Send Money in Nigeria and Have Bulletproof Legal Proof

When you send a bank transfer in Nigeria, the transaction receipt only proves that money moved from Account A to Account B. It does not prove what the money was for.

A transfer alert is cash evidence, not contract evidence. If you send ₦2,000,000 for a construction deposit and the supplier later claims it was a gift, a refund, or payment for a completely different job, the bank slip alone usually cannot settle the dispute. Courts need more than a number and an account. They need context, and context only comes from documentation you prepared before pressing send.

Why your bank receipt falls short

Bank receipts show the amount, date, sending account, receiving account, and sometimes a short narration. That is the full picture the bank provides. What they do not show is everything a court actually needs to resolve a dispute:

  • Receipt = payment only. The court can see that money moved, but not why. "Money moved" and "money was owed for this specific purpose" are two very different legal statements.
  • Narrations are weak. Transfer descriptions like "payment," "settlement," or "balance" are vague enough to mean almost anything. Without a signed agreement tying that narration to specific terms, the description carries little weight.
  • No contract details. Without a signed agreement, the receiver can claim the money was a personal loan, a gift, a refund of a previous debt, or payment for an entirely different service.
  • No identity link. The alert does not confirm that the person you intended to pay is the same person who controls the account that received the funds — a relevant gap when fraud or impersonation is alleged.

This is not a hypothetical problem. It plays out in small claims courts, magistrate courts, and police stations across Nigeria every week. Someone sends money, something goes wrong, and the only document they have is a bank alert that proves nothing beyond the movement of cash.

What good evidence looks like

The strongest evidence package for any significant payment is a written agreement signed before the money moves, plus a transfer narration that references that agreement directly. Together, they create a chain: this money was paid for this deal, to this person, on this date, under these specific terms.

When you can show a court a signed agreement and a matching transfer alert, you eliminate the most common defences a non-performing party uses. They cannot claim the money was for something else when the signed document says exactly what it was for. They cannot claim they never agreed to deliver when their signature is on the terms. They cannot claim the amount is wrong when the agreement and the alert show the same figure.

Court reality: Nigerian courts look for consistency across documents. A signed agreement, a matching transfer narration, and a WhatsApp confirmation of receipt form a consistent chain that is very hard to break. A lone bank alert, on the other hand, gives the opposing party room to construct an entirely different story.

Step-by-step: what to do before you press Send

  • Write the terms first. Create a short, clear agreement that describes exactly what is being paid for, the total amount, any instalment structure, the delivery or service date, and what happens if either party fails to perform. It does not need to be long — a half-page document with clear terms is more enforceable than a vague five-page template.
  • Get it signed before money moves. Both parties should confirm the agreement before a single naira is transferred. A signature — physical or verified digital — locks the terms. Once the money is sent, your negotiating leverage drops sharply.
  • Use a reference code in the narration. Put a contract number, invoice ID, or unique transaction reference in the bank transfer narration field and on the agreement itself. This creates a direct link between the payment and the document.
  • Verify the recipient's identity. Confirm the account name matches who you intend to pay. For any significant sum, use a method that ties the payment to a verifiable identity — phone number, NIN, or a platform that performs the verification for you before signing.
  • Save everything in one place. The signed agreement, the transfer alert, the bank confirmation, any invoices, and any supporting messages should all be stored together. If you ever need to present your case, you want to hand over a complete, consistent file — not a scattered collection of screenshots.

How to make it practical with an example

Say you are buying building materials from a supplier in Lagos. Before transferring anything, your agreement should say: "₦1,200,000 paid as first instalment for 500 bags of cement and 10 bundles of steel rods, to be delivered to [address] within 5 working days of payment. Balance of ₦800,000 due on confirmed delivery."

Then transfer the money with a narration that reads: "SIGNAM-INV-2026-017: Cement & Steel first instalment."

Now the supplier cannot claim the payment was for a generator. They cannot claim you sent it as a loan. They cannot claim the steel rods were not part of the deal. The agreement and the narration tell the same story, and that consistency is what courts rely on.

What if you already paid without proof?

If the payment is already done and you have no agreement in place, do not panic — but act quickly. Gather everything available: bank alerts, chat messages where the deal was discussed, any invoices or receipts the other party sent, and their contact and account details.

Then immediately send a written summary of what the payment was for and ask the recipient to confirm it in writing. This can be an email, a WhatsApp message you screenshot and keep, or a short document you both sign. Even a response of "yes, that's correct" in a message thread is better than nothing.

A post-payment written confirmation is weaker than a pre-payment signed agreement, but it still establishes intent and gives the other party less room to reframe the transaction. If they refuse to confirm the purpose of the payment, that refusal itself becomes relevant evidence.

The types of transactions where this matters most

Not every payment needs the same level of documentation. Buying suya on the street does not require a signed contract. But the following situations are where a payment without proper documentation most often leads to disputes in Nigeria:

  • Deposits for goods or services not yet delivered. The supplier has your money and has not yet performed. This is the highest-risk window, and it is exactly where a signed agreement with delivery terms protects you.
  • Loans to individuals. A transfer between two people with no agreement is almost impossible to enforce as a loan. The recipient can always claim it was a gift.
  • Freelance and contractor payments. Disputes over whether work was completed, whether it met the brief, and whether the payment was a deposit or full settlement are extremely common. A signed agreement with clear deliverables eliminates most of these arguments.
  • Rent and caution fees. Landlords sometimes deny receiving caution deposits or dispute the terms under which they are refundable. A signed receipt or tenancy agreement prevents this.
  • Business partnership contributions. Money paid into a joint venture without a clear agreement on ownership, roles, and profit sharing creates disputes that are notoriously difficult to resolve.

Why this matters in Nigeria specifically

Nigerian commercial disputes are often decided on consistency and credibility. A judge reviewing a contested payment will look at all available documents and ask: do these tell a coherent story? A signed agreement, a matching transfer narration, and a delivery confirmation create a consistent record that is very hard to challenge. A lone bank alert, on the other hand, leaves every important question open.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a bank transfer receipt enough proof of payment in Nigeria? +
A bank transfer receipt proves that money moved between two accounts, but it does not prove what the money was for. Without a signed agreement linking the payment to specific terms, the recipient can claim the money was a gift, loan, or unrelated debt.
What should I put in a bank transfer narration in Nigeria? +
Use a specific reference that ties the transfer to a signed agreement — for example, a contract number or invoice ID. Avoid vague narrations like "payment" or "settlement." A narration like "SIGNAM-INV-2026-017: Cement first instalment" creates a consistent, verifiable record that matches your agreement.
What can I do if I already paid without a contract? +
Gather every piece of evidence you have: bank alerts, chat messages, invoices, and delivery notes. Then send the other party a written confirmation of what the payment was for and ask them to acknowledge it in writing. A post-payment confirmation is weaker than a pre-payment agreement, but it is still far better than relying on an alert alone.
How do I verify who I am paying in Nigeria? +
Confirm the recipient's account name matches who you intend to pay. For significant transactions, use a platform that verifies the recipient's identity through NIN or SMS OTP before the agreement is signed, so there is an auditable link between the person and the payment.
