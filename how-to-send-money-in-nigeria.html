How to Send Money in Nigeria and Have Bulletproof Legal Proof

When you send a bank transfer in Nigeria, the transaction receipt only proves that money moved from Account A to Account B. It does not prove what the money was for.

A transfer alert is cash evidence, not contract evidence. If you send ₦2,000,000 for a construction deposit and the supplier later claims it was a gift, a refund, or payment for a different job, the bank slip alone usually cannot settle the dispute.

Why your bank receipt falls short

Bank receipts show amount, date, sending account, receiving account and sometimes a short narration. They do not show the agreed terms, the delivery timeline, the scope of the goods or service, or the risk allocation if one party fails to perform.

  • Receipt = payment only: the court can see that money moved, but not why.
  • Narrations are weak: transfer descriptions like "payment" or "settlement" are vague and can be disputed.
  • No contract details: without a signed agreement, the receiver can claim the money was a loan, gift, or unrelated debt.
  • No identity link: the alert does not confirm that the person you intended to pay is the same person who received the funds.

What good evidence looks like

The strongest evidence is a written agreement signed before payment, with a clear reference to the exact transfer. The agreement should name both parties, describe the transaction, state the amount, and include a date.

When the bank alert includes a reference number from that agreement, it becomes part of a consistent story: the money was paid for this specific deal, on this specific date, to this specific recipient.

Step-by-step before you press 'Send'

  • Write the terms first: create a short, clear agreement that describes what is being paid for, how much, and when delivery or service is due.
  • Use a reference code: put a contract number or unique transaction ID in the bank transfer narration and on the agreement itself.
  • Verify the recipient: confirm the account name, phone number, and ideally the recipient's identity with a secure method like SMS OTP or NIN verification.
  • Keep everything together: save the signed agreement, the transfer alert, bank confirmation, and any supporting invoices or messages in one folder.

How to make it practical

Example: if you are buying building materials, your agreement could say: "₦1,200,000 paid as first instalment for 500 bags of cement and 10 steel rods, to be delivered within 5 working days." Then transfer the money with narration: "SIGNAM-INV-2026-017: Cement first instalment."

This makes it difficult for the supplier to later claim the payment was for a generator, a loan, or an unrelated favour. The agreement and the transfer narration point to the same transaction.

What if you already paid without proof?

If the payment is already completed, do not panic. Gather every piece of evidence you have: bank alerts, chat messages, invoices, delivery notes, and account details. Then create a follow-up confirmation in writing and ask the recipient to acknowledge it.

Even after the fact, a written confirmation that both sides agree on the purpose of the payment improves your position. It may not be as strong as a prepayment agreement, but it is still better than relying on a lone transfer receipt.

Why this matters in Nigeria

Nigerian commercial disputes are often decided on consistency and credibility. Courts look for a documented chain of events. A signed agreement, a transfer narration, and a verification trail create a consistent record that judges can rely on.
