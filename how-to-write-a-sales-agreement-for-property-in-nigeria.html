How to Write a Sales Agreement for Property in Nigeria

Property fraud is one of the most common and devastating financial crimes in Nigeria. A properly written sales agreement is your first line of defence before any money leaves your account.

Every year, Nigerians lose millions of Naira to land and property transactions that were never properly documented — sellers who sell the same land to multiple buyers, agents who collect deposits without authority, and deals conducted entirely on handshakes and WhatsApp chats. A clear, signed sales agreement does not eliminate all risk, but it creates the legal foundation that allows you to recover your money or assert your rights if things go wrong.

What is a property sales agreement?

A property sales agreement — sometimes called a contract of sale — is a written document that records the terms under which a buyer and seller have agreed to transfer property. It is signed before or alongside the payment of a deposit or purchase price, and it commits both parties to completing the transaction under the stated terms.

It is important to understand what a sales agreement is not: it is not a title document, and it does not by itself transfer ownership. That requires a Deed of Assignment, which comes after full payment. The sales agreement is the contract that governs the transaction leading up to that point.

What a property sales agreement must include

A valid property sales agreement in Nigeria should contain the following elements:

  • Full identities of both parties: Legal names, addresses, and means of identification (NIN, passport, or driver's licence number) for both buyer and seller. If the seller is an agent, confirm they have a written authority to sell.
  • Property description: The full address, plot number, survey plan number, and any other identifying reference from the title documents. Do not rely on informal descriptions like "the plot behind the filling station."
  • Purchase price: The agreed total in Naira, in figures and words, and the currency in which payment will be made.
  • Payment structure: Whether the full amount is paid at once or in instalments — with deposit amount, balance, and due dates clearly stated.
  • Title documents: A list of the title documents the seller will hand over on completion — Certificate of Occupancy, Deed of Assignment, survey plan, or other applicable documents.
  • Completion date: The date by which the full purchase price must be paid and the Deed of Assignment must be executed.
  • Vacant possession: If the property is currently occupied, state the date by which vacant possession will be given to the buyer.
  • Default clause: What happens if either party fails to complete — typically, the seller refunds the deposit with damages if they default; the buyer forfeits the deposit if they pull out without cause.
  • Witnesses: At least one witness for each party, with their names, addresses, and signatures.
Before you sign anything: Verify that the seller's name on the agreement matches the name on the title documents. Ask to see the original Certificate of Occupancy or other title, and confirm the property is not encumbered by a mortgage, family dispute, or government acquisition notice. A sales agreement with a fraudulent seller is still a fraudulent transaction.

The difference between a sales agreement and a Deed of Assignment

Many buyers in Nigeria confuse these two documents. The sales agreement is the contract — it records what was agreed and commits both parties to the transaction. The Deed of Assignment is the conveyance — it is the document that legally passes ownership from the seller to the buyer.

The typical sequence is: sales agreement signed → deposit paid → balance paid → Deed of Assignment executed → Deed registered at the Land Registry. Skipping the sales agreement and going straight to a Deed of Assignment is unusual and risky, especially before full payment is confirmed.

Do you need a lawyer?

For land transactions — especially high-value ones — engaging a property lawyer is strongly advisable. They can verify the title, search for encumbrances, draft the agreement with the correct legal language, and ensure the Deed of Assignment is properly executed and registered.

However, the fundamental legal requirement is that the agreement captures the parties, the property, the price, and is signed by both parties. A digitally signed agreement that records each party's verified identity and the date of signing is a solid foundation, particularly for lower-value property sales or transactions between individuals who know each other.

Red flags that should stop you from signing

Walk away or pause immediately if:

  • The seller cannot produce original title documents — only photocopies.
  • The name on the title does not match the person claiming to sell.
  • You are told you must pay a deposit before seeing the agreement or documents.
  • The seller says the price is only available "today" and pressures you to sign immediately.
  • Multiple agents are offering the same property at different prices.
  • The seller refuses to allow you to take the documents to your own lawyer.

None of these alone is conclusive, but each one is a reason to slow down and verify before any money moves.

Protecting your deposit before the Deed is ready

In many Nigerian property transactions, there is a gap between the payment of a deposit and the completion of the formal Deed of Assignment. This gap is where disputes most often arise. A signed sales agreement that clearly documents the deposit amount, the balance due, and the completion deadline gives you legal standing to recover your money if the seller fails to complete.

Make every payment by bank transfer, never cash, and ensure the transfer narration references the property address or sales agreement. This creates a paper trail that connects your payment directly to the signed contract.
