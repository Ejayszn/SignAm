Freelance Service Contract Agreement Format for Nigerian Creatives

Every Nigerian freelancer has a horror story about a client who promised payment upon completion, only to stop replying to messages once the work was delivered.

In the world of web design, copywriting, and app development, the difference between a paid job and a ghosted project is often one sentence: the contract. A clear service agreement turns casual chat into a measurable commitment, so you can invoice, deliver, and follow up from a position of strength.

Why a formal freelance agreement matters in Nigeria

When you work without a written contract, you are relying on memory, good faith, and the hope that the client remembers every promise. That is not a strategy. A written agreement gives you:

  • proof of what was agreed, including scope, timeline and payment;
  • a clear payment structure so you are not waiting weeks for a final balance;
  • boundaries around revisions, delivery formats, and final handover;
  • a record of who owns the work and when ownership changes hands.

The Evidence Act 2011 recognises electronic records if they can be authenticated and shown to be reliable. A digitally signed agreement with verified contact details and an audit trail carries far more weight than a screenshot from WhatsApp.

Key Terms Your Freelance Contract Must Contain

  • The Deposit Threshold: Never start work without a locked commitment, typically 30–50% upfront, depending on the project size.
  • Milestone Sign-offs: Divide the job into stages and tie payments to deliverables, so you get paid as the project moves forward.
  • IP Ownership Transfer: Spell out that the copyright, source files, and final deliverables are released only after the final payment is cleared.
  • Revision Limits: State how many review rounds are included and what extra revisions will cost.
  • Cancellation and Delay Terms: Define what happens if the client cancels, changes scope, or delays feedback.

The Deposit Threshold is the first line of defence against ghosting. Ask for at least a partial payment before you book time or hand over drafts. If a client refuses, the safest response is to say the work cannot begin until the deposit is received.

Milestone Sign-offs keep large projects from becoming a single open-ended job. For example, if you are building a website, split it into design, development, review, and launch. Each completed phase should have a simple sign-off so the client cannot claim they never approved the work.

IP Ownership Transfer should never be implied. A contract that says "copyright passes on full payment" means you still own the work until the client settles the invoice. This protects you if the client wants to resell the design or ask for later changes without paying.

Revision Limits prevent scope creep. Many projects derail because the client expects unlimited small changes. If your agreement includes two rounds of revisions, make that explicit and state how much each extra round costs.

Cancellation and Delay Terms save you from being stuck with unpaid work. Write what happens if the client cancels after work begins, or if they delay feedback for more than a week. A simple clause that restores part of the deposit in those cases is often enough.

How to draft your agreement clearly

Use plain language. Avoid legalese. The goal is that both you and the client should understand the same thing without having to translate it.

  • Start with the parties: your full name or business name, and the client's full name or business name.
  • Define the work: be specific about deliverables, formats, platform, and deadline.
  • State payment terms: deposit amount, milestone payments, final balance, and accepted payment methods.
  • Outline revision rules: number of rounds, response time, and the fee for extras.
  • List what you need from the client: assets, feedback, login details, or approvals.

Attach the project scope as a short schedule or appendix rather than leaving it in your WhatsApp chat. This makes the agreement easier to enforce and easier to revise if the client asks for changes.

Why WhatsApp agreements don't protect you

Many Nigerian freelancers rely on WhatsApp chats as their "contract." The problem is that chat logs can be deleted, screenshots can be altered, and a client can simply claim the messages were taken out of context. A signed digital agreement with a verified phone number attached to it is fundamentally harder to dispute.

WhatsApp is great for quick communication, but it is not a legal record. A proper agreement keeps the terms in one place, shows when each party accepted them, and prevents the conversation from being rewritten after the fact.
