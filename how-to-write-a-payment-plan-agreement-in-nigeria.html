How to Write a Payment Plan Agreement in Nigeria

When a debtor cannot pay the full amount at once, a payment plan agreement is the difference between recovering your money in structured instalments and recovering nothing at all.

Many creditors in Nigeria make the mistake of accepting a verbal promise to pay "small small" — without documenting the arrangement. Three weeks later, the first instalment is late. A month after that, the debtor is no longer picking up calls. A properly written payment plan agreement prevents that situation by converting the repayment commitment into an enforceable contract with clear consequences for default.

What is a payment plan agreement?

A payment plan agreement — also called an instalment agreement or repayment schedule — is a contract between a creditor and a debtor that sets out how an existing debt will be repaid over time. It acknowledges the total amount owed, breaks it into scheduled payments, and specifies the consequences if the debtor misses a payment.

Importantly, signing a payment plan agreement can also serve as a fresh acknowledgement of the original debt, which can restart the legal limitation period and give you renewed standing to pursue the full amount in court if necessary.

When should you offer a payment plan?

A payment plan is appropriate when:

  • The debtor acknowledges the debt but genuinely cannot pay the full amount immediately.
  • The full amount is unlikely to be recovered quickly through court action.
  • You want to preserve the relationship while still recovering your money.
  • You are restructuring an existing loan or debt arrangement.

It is not appropriate when the debtor denies the debt, when they have a history of missing agreed payments, or when the evidence suggests they are buying time rather than genuinely intending to pay.

What your payment plan agreement must include

A payment plan that will hold up legally in Nigeria should contain the following elements:

  • Parties: Full legal names, addresses, and phone numbers of both the creditor and the debtor.
  • Total amount owed: The exact outstanding balance in Naira, in both figures and words.
  • Origin of the debt: A brief description of what the debt is for — a loan, unpaid invoice, advance payment, or other obligation.
  • Instalment schedule: A clear table or list showing each payment date and the exact amount due. Be specific — "on the 15th of each month" is stronger than "monthly."
  • Payment method: The bank account details or payment method the debtor must use, so there is no ambiguity about where the money should go.
  • Interest clause (if applicable): If you are charging interest on the outstanding balance, state the annual rate, how it is calculated, and include it in the instalment amounts.
  • Default clause: What happens if the debtor misses a payment. Typically, this clause states that one missed payment accelerates the entire remaining balance — the full outstanding amount becomes immediately due and the creditor may pursue legal recovery without further notice.
  • Signatures: Both parties must sign and date the agreement. The debtor's signature is essential — without it, the document is only your intention, not a mutual commitment.
Key clause to include: "In the event that any scheduled payment is missed or delayed by more than [X] days, the full outstanding balance shall become immediately due and payable, and [Creditor's Name] reserves the right to pursue recovery of the total amount through legal proceedings without further notice."

Sample instalment schedule format

Below is a simple format for structuring the repayment schedule within the agreement:

Total Amount Owed: ₦360,000 (Three Hundred and Sixty Thousand Naira)

Repayment Schedule:

Payment Due Date Amount
1st Instalment15 July 2026₦120,000
2nd Instalment15 August 2026₦120,000
3rd Instalment15 September 2026₦120,000

Is a payment plan agreement legally binding?

Yes, provided both parties sign it voluntarily and the terms are clear. A signed payment plan is a fresh contract that acknowledges the debt and commits the debtor to a specific repayment structure. If the debtor defaults, you do not need to relitigate whether the original debt exists — the payment plan agreement itself is the evidence that they confirmed the debt and agreed to repay it.

A digitally signed agreement with a timestamp and verified identity is even stronger, because the debtor cannot later claim the signature was forged or that they did not understand what they were signing.

What to do when a debtor misses a payment

Act immediately. Do not wait for the next payment date hoping the debtor will catch up. Send a written notice on the same day the payment was due, referencing the payment plan agreement and the default clause. This documents the breach and triggers your right to pursue the full outstanding balance.

From that point, follow the debt recovery process: a formal Letter of Demand, then court action if necessary. Your signed payment plan agreement, combined with bank records showing which payments were made and which were missed, gives you an extremely clear case to present to a magistrate.
