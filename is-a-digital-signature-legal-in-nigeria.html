Is a Digital Signature Legally Binding in Nigeria?

Many people in Nigeria still believe an agreement is only valid if it features a blue-ink physical signature and a red wax seal. This is entirely false.

Under Section 93 of the Nigerian Evidence Act 2011, electronic and digital signatures are recognized and treated as valid evidence in the same way as handwritten signatures. The law is clear: what matters is the intent of the signer and the reliability of the electronic record.

That means a properly created digital contract can be enforced in court just like a paper contract — provided it is supported by strong identity proof and a reliable audit trail.

Digital signature vs electronic signature

In plain language, an electronic signature is any sound, symbol or process attached to an electronic record that shows agreement. A digital signature is a secure form of electronic signature that uses cryptographic methods to lock the document.

In Nigeria, both can be legally binding. The key issue is not the label, but whether the system proves who signed, when they signed, and whether the contract remained unchanged afterwards.

What makes a digital contract valid in a Nigerian court?

Section 84 of the Evidence Act sets out what makes a computer-generated record trustworthy. A digital agreement should answer three practical questions:

  • Who signed it? The system must link the signature to a real person, not just a device or an email address.
  • Was the document altered? The record should show the contract in its final state and preserve that state after signing.
  • Can the signer deny it? There should be evidence that the signer knowingly approved the contract, such as OTP verification or authenticated login.

These are not legal buzzwords. They are the practical safeguards judges look for when deciding whether to rely on electronic evidence.

How evidence is evaluated

A judge does not simply accept any screenshot or forwarded message. The court will ask:

  • Can the record be linked to a verified identity?
  • Is there a trustworthy chain of custody for the document?
  • Was consent given voluntarily and with knowledge of the terms?

If the answer to any of these questions is no, the agreement risks being treated as unreliable or informal.

Are WhatsApp agreements admissible in court?

Yes, WhatsApp messages can be admitted as evidence, but they are the weakest form of proof. Courts in Nigeria have accepted WhatsApp chats in some cases, but only when there is supporting evidence that confirms the sender's identity and the context of the conversation.

The problem with WhatsApp is simple:

  • Messages can be copied and altered.
  • Accounts can be impersonated.
  • There is no built-in way to prove the signer saw the final terms and consciously agreed.

For casual offers, WhatsApp is fine. For binding contracts, it should be a starting point, not the final proof.

What about email agreements?

Emails are stronger than chat messages because they create a record with sender headers and timestamps. Still, email alone usually does not prove who typed the message or whether the sender actually approved the exact contract terms.

When email is combined with an identity check — such as phone-based OTP, linked NIN verification, or a secure login — it becomes far more reliable. That is why the best digital agreements use multiple layers of proof.

How to make your digital agreement enforceable

To make an electronic document enforceable in Nigeria, follow these practical steps:

  • Capture the signer's identity with a trustworthy method, such as phone verification or NIN lookup.
  • Keep the exact signed document intact and store it securely in a tamper-proof system.
  • Record the date, time, device, and location of the signing event.
  • Keep an audit trail that shows each step: invitation, review, consent, and final signature.

Without these steps, the electronic record is simply a piece of evidence. With them, it becomes a contract that a court can rely on.
