What to Do When Someone Owes You Money in Nigeria and Refuses to Pay

In Nigeria, lending money or doing business on credit is easy; the challenge is recovering it when the other side suddenly stops replying.

Whether it is a neighbour who promised to settle the balance after salary, a vendor who collected a deposit and disappeared, or a client who accepted work and refused to pay, the feeling is the same: frustrated and stuck. The truth is that the legal path is available, but it only works when you combine the right evidence with a calm, deliberate process.

Can I involve the Police if someone owes me money?

Most people think the police are the first stop. In reality, the Nigerian Police Force is not a debt collection agency. Ordinary cases of unpaid loans or broken agreements are civil matters, and the police usually refer them to the courts.

There is one important exception: if the debtor used fake credentials, forged transfer alerts, or knowingly took payment for goods or services they never had, it can become a criminal offence under Obtaining Under False Pretences. In that scenario, you can file a criminal petition, but only after you have preserved the evidence and can show it was a deliberate fraud.

Step 1: Build a complete evidence file before you act

Your strongest position is the one with documents, timestamps and consistent records. The court does not care about your sense of betrayal. It cares about proof that a debt existed and was not paid.

Collect everything, including:

  • Bank transfer receipts with the transaction date, amount, beneficiary name and narration.
  • WhatsApp, SMS or email exchanges where the debtor accepts the debt amount, delivery terms, or a promise to pay by a certain date.
  • Written invoices, purchase orders, delivery notes, or signed paper agreements.
  • Screenshots of phone calls or messages confirming the debt, especially when the debtor agrees to instalment plans or repayment dates.

If the debtor blocks you, preserve the last screen before the block or screenshot the chat thread. Do not delete the conversation. Keep the metadata intact if possible, because a screening process will verify the full history.

Important: Avoid public shaming on social media. Posting the debtor's details can expose you to defamation or cyberbullying claims under Nigerian law, and it undermines your credibility in court. Keep the matter private and professional.

Step 2: Send a formal Letter of Demand

A Letter of Demand is a simple but powerful tool. It moves the dispute from casual chat into a documented legal notice, and many debtors respond quickly once they realise you are serious.

Your letter should include:

  • The exact amount owed, including any agreed fees or interest.
  • A short description of what was supplied and when.
  • A clear due date for payment, usually 7 to 14 days after delivery.
  • A statement that you will pursue court action if payment is not received.
  • Contact details and the preferred payment method.

Send the Letter of Demand through a means you can prove: email with read receipt, registered post, or a delivery note via a trusted courier. Keep the proof of delivery, because the court may ask whether the debtor actually received the notice.

If the debtor contacts you and offers a partial payment arrangement, record it in writing immediately. A new agreement is better than no recovery at all.

Step 3: File in Small Claims Court if necessary

Once the demand period passes, the next step is the court that exists precisely for this situation. Small Claims Courts in states like Lagos, Abuja and Oyo are designed to resolve consumer and contract disputes quickly, often without requiring a lawyer.

Typical steps in the small claims process:

  • Prepare a statement of claim describing the transaction and what remains unpaid.
  • Attach copies of your evidence bundle: bank alerts, chats, invoices and any agreement.
  • Pay the filing fee and submit to the court registry. The amount depends on the value of the claim.
  • Attend the hearing. Both parties explain their case, and the magistrate may order settlement or deliver a judgment within weeks.

These courts are meant to be fast. If the claim is valid, judgment can arrive in 30 to 60 days. After judgment, the court can issue enforcement orders such as garnishee proceedings or seizure of assets. That is the moment when the debtor can no longer simply ignore you.

What if the debtor refuses to cooperate after judgment?

A court judgment is a legal right, but enforcement can still take effort. If the debtor ignores the order, you may need to apply for a warrant of execution or a garnishee order through the magistrate's office. The court clerk can explain the local process, and often the mere step of enforcing the judgment is enough to prompt payment.

Remember: the longer you wait, the harder it becomes. If you are sure the debt is valid, act quickly. A well-documented claim in small claims court is usually more effective than repeated emotional appeals.

How to protect your money from the start

The best time to secure payment is before the first naira leaves your account. When you rely on verbal promises or informal chats, you are giving the debtor room to deny the agreement later.

Use written agreements with clear terms: amounts, delivery dates, repayment schedules, and consequences for late payment. Better still, use a secure digital contract that links signatures to verified credentials like the debtor's NIN and phone number.

When a contract is created through a platform that records the identity verification trail, you get a stronger record. In a Nigerian court, that means the agreement is harder to deny and quicker to enforce.

Keep the process calm and professional

Emotion is natural, but anger is rarely helpful. If the debtor starts giving reasons, ask for them in writing and respond with a clear next step. If they insist they cannot pay, they must still put that in writing. That becomes part of your evidence.

Take control by setting deadlines, keeping records, and using the legal tools available in Nigeria rather than relying on hope. This is the path that turns a frustrating situation into a recoverable one.
